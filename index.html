John D. Morgan, Ph.D., GISP

I am an associate professor teaching and have been the primary faculty within the MS GIS Admin program for over a decade. at the University of West Florida. While completing my PhD in Geography from Florida State University, my GIS career began in industry and public-sector technology roles, including systems engineering, web development, and GIS application development for the Florida House of Representatives’ Redistricting Committee. Since transitioning to academia, I have held research and teaching appointments at UNC Asheville and Western Carolina University, with a sustained focus on preparing students for professional GIS careers in government, emergency management, and environmental analysis.

My research focuses on the development and application of geospatial methods to support spatial decision-making related to environmental hazards, public safety, land use, and infrastructure systems. Drawing from GIScience, cartography, and geospatial computing, I examine how spatial data, models, and visualizations can improve understanding of risk, access, and governance across dynamic landscapes.

Methodologically, my research advances geospatial computing and GeoAI, including the development of satellite-derived datasets, Python-based analytical workflows, spatial databases, and deep-learning models for hazard detection and damage assessment. Recent projects include benchmark Sentinel-2 datasets and scalable pipelines for wildfire mapping.

I also maintain active research programs in spatial criminology and legal geography. This work examines crime patterns, policing activity, and environmental enforcement using crime mapping, time-geographic analysis, and GeoAI, as well as the spatial dimensions of property rights, access, and regulation through participatory mapping and viewshed analysis. Across all areas, cartography and geovisualization serve as core analytical and communication tools.

I continue to maintain my Geographic Information Systems Professional (GISP) credentials. Also, I am proud to have published three chapters in the UCGIS Geographic Information Science & Technology Body of Knowledge (BoK), such as the topics of spatial joins and crime mapping.
