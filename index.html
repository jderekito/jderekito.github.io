John D. Morgan, Ph.D., GISP

I am an associate professor teaching primarily within the MS GIS Admin program at the University of West Florida.

My research focuses on the development and application of geospatial methods to support spatial decision-making related to environmental hazards, public safety, land use, and infrastructure systems. Drawing from GIScience, cartography, and geospatial computing, I examine how spatial data, models, and visualizations can improve understanding of risk, access, and governance across dynamic landscapes.

A central theme of my work is hazard preparedness and response, particularly in coastal and forested environments affected by hurricanes, wildfires, and other extreme events. I have contributed to studies analyzing evacuation zones, power outages, wildfire damage, and land-use change, emphasizing how spatial patterns of exposure and amenity influence communities, infrastructure, and property systems. This work is intended to support emergency management, planning, and policy evaluation through reproducible, data-driven approaches.

Methodologically, my research advances geospatial computing and increasingly GeoAI, including the development of satellite-derived datasets, Python-based workflows, and deep-learning models for hazard detection and damage assessment. Recent work includes the creation of benchmark Sentinel-2 datasets for wildfire mapping, experimentation with deep-learning architectures, and the integration of spatial databases and analytical pipelines to support scalable geospatial analysis.

I also maintain a sustained interest in legal geography and spatial governance, particularly where property rights, access, and regulatory boundaries intersect with physical space. Through participatory mapping, viewshed analysis, and spatial indices, my work examines how legal frameworks and territorial practices shape public access, land use, and conflict, with applications in coastal environments and public spaces.

Across these areas, cartography and geovisualization play a foundational role. I design and evaluate maps and web-based visualizations as analytical tools, emphasizing usability, transparency, and communication for diverse audiences, including planners, emergency managers, and the public.

Overall, my research contributes integrative geospatial methods that bridge technical innovation with applied problem-solving, supporting informed decision-making around hazards, infrastructure, and spatial governance.

I continue to maintain my Geographic Information Systems Professional (GISP) credentials. Also, I am proud to have published three chapters in the UCGIS Geographic Information Science & Technology Body of Knowledge (BoK), such as the topics of spatial joins and crime mapping.
